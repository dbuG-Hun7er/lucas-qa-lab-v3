HISTORY OF AN ENIGMA
Before getting into the main body of our material, a brief survey of the subject of UFOs is
needed. It may surprise some readers to learn that the phenomenon of UFOs appears to be as old as
history. Indeed, long before man himself could fly, people were seeing unexplained craft in the
heavens.
Of course, human nature being what it is, we have always tended to interpret celestial sights
according to our own world-views. At one time, comets were regarded as omens of ill fortune; even
though we know understand that they are a simple celestial phenomenon. The same thing could be
said of lunar and solar eclipses. Such wonders once inspired terror and dread, but now—at least
among modern peoples, they are just fascinating celestial events.
However, not all of the ancient sights in the sky were that easily explained. Many of the ancient
peoples were fairly savvy about things astronomical (for example, the Magi who predicted our Lord’s
birth by observing a stellar event). Yet, even some of the wisest ancient chroniclers still were baffled
by some of the things they observed in the skies.
Glowing, wondrous craft in the heavens were reported by ancient writers as early as 90 B.C.
through the 1st-2nd century A.D. UFOs were mentioned by Pliny and Seneca, and also Herodian in
170 A.D. Given that the speculations of writers like Erich von Daaniken are pretty fanciful,3 there is
still a lot of ancient material pointing to UFO sightings.
The Venerable Bede’s (673-735) History of England mentions them; and interestingly enough
Columbus reported sighting them in what today we call the Bermuda Triangle on October 11, 1492;
one day before he discovered the New World. Of course, few if any of these observers thought what
they were seeing were spacecraft. But they often described them as “flying ships” of one sort or
another.
In a more sinister context, during the outbreaks of the bubonic plague In the Middle Ages, odd
comets were often sighted right before an outbreak of the horrible disease. These particular “comets”
were shaped like what we would call a dirigible today and even had PORTHOLES. Often, these
ancient writers would associate these flying craft with either divine or diabolical influences. They
might not have been far off.
